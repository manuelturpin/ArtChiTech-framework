# Circle of Competence

> **"Know your circle of competence, and stick within it. The size of that circle is not very important; knowing its boundaries, however, is vital."** â€” Warren Buffett

## What Is It?

The Circle of Competence describes the areas where you have genuine expertise versus those where you don't. Success comes from operating within your circle while honestly acknowledging its edges. Expand it deliberately, but never pretend it's larger than it is.

## When to Use

- Before taking on new challenges
- When making decisions outside your expertise
- Hiring and delegation decisions
- Investment decisions
- Knowing when to ask for help
- Self-assessment and growth planning

## When NOT to Use

- As an excuse to avoid growth
- When the situation demands action despite uncertainty
- When your circle is clearly sufficient

---

## Process

### Step 1: Define Your Circles
Map what you truly know vs. what you think you know.

### Step 2: Be Honest About Boundaries
Where does genuine expertise end?

### Step 3: Identify the Decision Zone
Is this decision inside, on the edge, or outside?

### Step 4: Act Accordingly
- Inside: Proceed with confidence
- Edge: Proceed with caution, seek input
- Outside: Delegate or learn first

### Step 5: Expand Deliberately
Build competence through deliberate practice.

## Expanding Your Circle

The circle should grow over time, but deliberately:

1. **Adjacent areas** â€” Expand from where you're strong
2. **Deliberate practice** â€” Not just exposure, but focused improvement
3. **Feedback** â€” Get honest assessment of your progress
4. **Humility** â€” Expanding takes longer than you think

### The 10,000-Hour Reality
True competence takes years. Beware of:
- "I read a book about it" (you're still outside)
- "I did it once" (you're on the edge at best)
- "It seems straightforward" (Dunning-Kruger alert)

---

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Overestimating your circle** â€” Most common mistake
2. **Refusing to expand** â€” Staying forever in comfort zone
3. **Expertise transfer illusion** â€” Being expert in A doesn't make you expert in related B
4. **Confidence as evidence** â€” Feeling certain â‰  being right
5. **Underestimating circle** â€” Impostor syndrome; you may know more than you think
